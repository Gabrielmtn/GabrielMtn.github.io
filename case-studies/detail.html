Virgin Pulse

UX Design Engineer · Leading Accessibility Strategy

Sector
Enterprise Wellness
Requirement
Section 508 / Bank of America
Scope
300+ engineers · org-wide SOP
The fundamental
We don't control how content is consumed. Users do.
Andrés Galante, Accessible Font Sizing, Explained

Virgin Pulse hired me into the product org for a specific reason: Bank of America, one of the biggest clients, had been promised Section 508 compliance, and the product and engineering orgs were struggling to find leadership internally. My background sits at the intersection of front-end engineering and accessibility, which is exactly where that problem lives.

The strategy was shift-left before shift-left existed. Instead of treating WCAG as a QA gate, we moved accessibility upstream into how features were designed, built, and reviewed. I worked directly with engineering and product leadership to define the standard, then with feature teams to make it real in their code. We designed annotation tools and training programs so teams could design for Reflow (1.4.10) and Resize Text (1.4.4), not treat it like an afterthought.

The spectrum

Four approaches to responsive layout. Only one holds up when users zoom or resize.

This was a core element of our training. Most teams were shipping the fixed example, or at best the fluid example, and calling it responsive. The fourth is the one that meets WCAG and scales.

Fixed

Pixel-set widths. Breaks at zoom. Overflows narrow viewports.

Fluid

Scales continuously. Typography breaks at extremes (lines too long, then too short).

Adaptive

Jumps at preset breakpoints. Works fine in between but brittle at the thresholds.

Fluid + Breakpoints

Fluid within bands. Reflows at thresholds. The only approach that meets WCAG 1.4.10 acceptance criteria at all zoom levels.

Diagram from the training deck Engineering Responsiveness Concepts and Training, delivered org-wide at Virgin Pulse.

The work

Building the approach into standards, code, and training.

  1. Co-authored Virgin Pulse's WCAG AA strategy to meet the Section 508 requirement on the Bank of America contract, defining accessible patterns and component standards adopted organization-wide.
  2. Led a proof of concept refactoring Angular micro-frontend templates and CSS to meet Reflow (1.4.10) and Resize Text (1.4.4). The approach was adopted as the engineering SOP across 300+ engineers.
  3. Designed and delivered two accessibility training curricula (one for engineers, one for designers and product leads) built on relative units, fluid grids, and container-guided layout.
Outcome
400+ Engineers, designers, and PMs trained
300+ Engineers operating on the new SOP
§508 Enterprise contract requirement met

№ 02 / Component & Craft / 2017

Putnam Investments

Accessibility Engineer · Component refactoring for WCAG AA compliance

Sector
Financial Services
Standard
WCAG AA compliance
Scope
Combobox · Menus · Dialogs · Page flow
The Fundamental
Build the consistency keyboard users need, first.
Working principle

This was a massive accessibility refactor for a financial services website. Dozens of interactive components (comboboxes, menus, dialogs, disclosure widgets) needed to be rebuilt from the ground up. This is where I learned how to build accessible components by reading WCAG documentation and understanding keyboard interaction patterns.

In 2017, the ARIA Authoring Practices Guide didn't exist in its current form. I worked directly from the ARIA spec and WCAG keyboard interaction patterns. Screen reader users are keyboard users first. They navigate with arrow keys, Enter, Escape, and Tab just like sighted keyboard users. I learned to implement the keyboard behavior first, then add ARIA roles to communicate state. Every component was tested in JAWS and NVDA before it shipped.

The anatomy

A combobox, built from WCAG keyboard patterns.

I learned to build comboboxes by reading the WCAG keyboard interaction documentation. Despite the Authoring Practice Guide not existing yet, the patterns were clear: arrow keys navigate options, Enter selects, Escape closes and returns focus. I implemented the keyboard behavior first, then added ARIA roles to communicate state. This spec shows what that looked like.

Component spec derived from the ARIA 1.0 / 1.1 specs and validated against JAWS and NVDA before ship. One of dozens in the Putnam refactor set.

The work

Building from WCAG patterns, validating with real users.

  1. Started with WCAG keyboard interaction documentation for each component type. Implemented arrow key navigation, Enter/Escape behavior, and focus management exactly as the patterns specified. Added ARIA roles to communicate state to screen readers after the keyboard behavior was correct.
  2. Treated page structure as a navigation system. Landmark regions, heading hierarchy, and tab order had to work together. A keyboard user's mental map depends on all three being consistent. Fix one in isolation and the others break.
  3. Made focus management part of the component design. Dialogs return focus to the trigger on close. Composite widgets use roving tabindex. Modal focus traps are intentional and escapable. Tested every pattern by navigating with keyboard only before adding screen reader validation.
  4. Validated every component in JAWS and NVDA before shipping. If the keyboard interaction was predictable, screen readers announced it correctly. If keyboard behavior was wrong, no amount of ARIA could fix it.

№ 03 / Research & Velocity / 2019

Akamai Technologies

Sr. UI/UX Developer & Designer · Hybrid design/development role

Sector
Infosec / Federal Cloud
Requirement
FedRAMP remediation tracker
Approach
Stakeholder collaboration · Design + build
The fundamental
Watch how people work, not just what they say.
Working principle

This was my first hybrid design/development role. The team needed a FedRAMP remediation tracker, and three different groups would use it: compliance officers, security engineers, and managers. Each group had their own vocabulary and way of thinking about the work.

I spent time with eight stakeholders, watching how they actually did their jobs. The contextual inquiry showed that they all tracked the same vulnerabilities but talked about them completely differently. The design challenge was creating one tool that worked for all three groups without forcing anyone to learn a new way of thinking.

The anatomy

Understanding stakeholder needs through observation.

I embedded with compliance officers, engineers, and managers to understand how they worked. Each group used different terms for the same concepts. The card sorting exercise helped me see where their vocabularies overlapped and where they diverged. That informed the information architecture.

Synthesis diagram showing three role-specific mental models feeding one shared IA. The wireframe above is representative of the shipped structure, not a screenshot.

The work

Collaborating with stakeholders to design and build the tool.

  1. Spent time with eight stakeholders (three compliance officers, three security engineers, two managers) watching how they tracked vulnerabilities in their daily work. Observed first, asked questions second.
  2. Built a custom card-sorting tool when procurement timelines would have delayed the project. Used it to understand how each group organized and labeled the same remediation concepts. The patterns showed where vocabularies aligned and where they conflicted.
  3. Created three rounds of prototypes and tested them with the same eight stakeholders. Each iteration refined how the interface presented information to accommodate different mental models without forcing anyone to translate.
  4. Designed and developed the tool in Ruby on Rails with ERB templates and JavaScript. The information architecture used terminology from all three groups, making the tool feel native to compliance, engineering, and management perspectives.
Outcome

03 Iteration rounds with the operating team
FedRAMP vulnerability tracking tool deployed
